Solar Energy: Technologies, Current Status, and Future Prospects

An overview of how we harvest sunlight today—through photovoltaics, solar thermal systems and solar-to-hydrogen technologies—how far we have come, and where we are headed.

1. Introduction to Solar Energy

Solar energy is the collection and conversion of the Sun’s radiation into useful forms of energy such as electricity, heat, or chemical fuels. In a sense, it is the original energy source for almost everything on Earth: climate systems, plant growth, and even fossil fuels all trace back to sunlight stored in different ways over time.

At the top of Earth’s atmosphere, the Sun delivers on the order of 173,000 terawatts of power, several thousand times more than current human energy use. Only a portion of this reaches the surface, and an even smaller part can be practically harvested, but the underlying resource is effectively inexhaustible on human time scales.

Solar energy has several defining characteristics:

  • High availability but modest power density: bright sunlight at noon on a clear day provides about 1,000 watts per square meter, but when averaged over day–night cycles, seasons, and clouds, the usable power density is much lower.
  • Variability: output fluctuates predictably with day and season, and unpredictably with weather, requiring flexibility elsewhere in the energy system.
  • Decentralisation potential: solar can be deployed on rooftops, in village microgrids, or in multi-gigawatt utility-scale plants, giving it unusual versatility.

Over the last two decades, rapid cost declines, supportive policies and concern over climate change have transformed solar from a niche technology into one of the central pillars of the global energy transition.

2. Applications of Solar Energy

Because sunlight can be converted both to heat and to electricity (and even to chemical fuels), solar energy is used across many sectors of the economy.

2.1 Electricity generation

The most visible application is grid-connected electricity from solar photovoltaics (PV). Modern PV systems range from a few kilowatts on home rooftops to utility-scale solar farms of several gigawatts. Key use cases include:

  • Residential and commercial rooftop systems
  • Community solar projects that share one plant among many users
  • Large ground-mounted solar farms, often with single- or dual-axis tracking
  • Floating solar installations on reservoirs and lakes
  • Off-grid systems for rural electrification, telecom towers and remote facilities

2.2 Heating and cooling

Solar thermal systems collect sunlight as heat rather than electricity. They are widely used for:

  • Solar water heating for homes, hotels, hospitals and swimming pools, using flat-plate or evacuated-tube collectors.
  • Space heating via hydronic or air-based systems that preheat building air or water.
  • Industrial process heat at low to medium temperatures for food processing, textiles, and other sectors.
  • Solar cooling, where solar heat drives absorption or adsorption chillers.

2.3 Transport and mobility

Solar power also supports cleaner transport, mainly indirectly:

  • Charging electric vehicles with PV-generated electricity
  • Producing green hydrogen and synthetic fuels (e-fuels) for aviation, shipping and heavy road transport
  • Niche applications such as solar-assisted boats, drones, or experimental aircraft

2.4 Agriculture and water

In agriculture and water management, solar technologies are increasingly used to:

  • Power irrigation pumps and village-scale water supply
  • Drive desalination and water purification systems
  • Dry crops, fruits and fish using solar dryers
  • Stabilise temperatures in greenhouses and livestock buildings

3. Methods of Harvesting Solar Energy

Most practical solar technologies fall into three broad categories: photovoltaics (PV), solar thermal systems, and solar-to-chemical pathways that produce fuels such as hydrogen.

3.1 Photovoltaics (PV)

Photovoltaic devices convert light directly into electricity through the photovoltaic effect. When photons with sufficient energy strike a semiconductor, they excite electrons into higher energy states, creating charge carriers that generate electric current when driven through an external circuit.

Modern PV technologies include:

  • Crystalline silicon (c-Si): The dominant commercial technology, responsible for over 90% of today’s module production. Standard modules achieve around 20–23% efficiency, with advanced designs such as PERC, TOPCon and heterojunction cells pushing the limits of single-junction silicon.
  • Thin-film PV: Technologies such as cadmium telluride (CdTe) and copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS) use very thin absorbing layers on glass or flexible substrates. They offer lightweight and building-integrated options, though with smaller market share.
  • Perovskite and tandem cells: Metal halide perovskites have quickly become a leading research topic thanks to their tunable bandgaps and high efficiencies. Stacked perovskite–silicon tandem cells capture more of the solar spectrum and have reached certified research efficiencies around 34–35%. Commercial perovskite–silicon tandem panels are now emerging with efficiencies around 25% or higher.
  • Organic, dye-sensitized and building-integrated PV (BIPV): These approaches prioritise flexibility, colour and transparency, making it possible to integrate solar cells into windows, curtain walls, facades and roofing materials.

A PV system includes modules, inverters, mounting structures (sometimes with sun-tracking), cabling, safety equipment, and often monitoring and storage. The cost of complete systems has fallen dramatically, making solar one of the cheapest options for new power generation in many parts of the world.

3.2 Solar thermal technologies

Solar thermal systems collect sunlight as heat, which can be used directly or converted into electricity.

  • Low- and medium-temperature collectors: Flat-plate and evacuated-tube collectors provide hot water or air up to roughly 80–150 °C, widely used in buildings and some industrial processes.
  • Concentrating solar power (CSP): Mirrors such as parabolic troughs, heliostat fields feeding central towers, or linear Fresnel systems concentrate sunlight to achieve 400–1,000 °C or more. This heat drives conventional steam turbines or other engines.
  • Thermal energy storage: CSP plants often use molten salt or other storage media to hold several hours of heat, allowing them to generate electricity after sunset and provide valuable flexibility to the grid.

3.3 Solar-to-chemical: photocatalysis and hydrogen production

Solar energy can also be stored in chemical bonds by producing fuels such as hydrogen. This is attractive because hydrogen can be stored over long periods and used in sectors where direct electrification is difficult, such as steelmaking or long-distance shipping.

3.3.1 Photocatalytic (PC) water splitting

In photocatalytic water splitting, semiconductor particles or films absorb sunlight and directly drive the splitting of water into hydrogen and oxygen. Light generates electron–hole pairs in the catalyst; electrons reduce protons to hydrogen while holes oxidise water to oxygen.

Research focuses on developing photocatalysts that are:

  • Efficient under visible light
  • Stable in water over long operating times
  • Made from abundant, non-toxic materials

Although there has been significant progress in materials and reactor design, solar-to-hydrogen efficiencies in practical systems remain modest, and large-scale deployment is still at the research and pilot stage.

3.3.2 Photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting

Photoelectrochemical cells combine light absorbers with electrochemical interfaces. A photoelectrode generates a photovoltage under illumination; in contact with an electrolyte and suitable catalysts, this drives water splitting into hydrogen and oxygen.

Compared with particulate photocatalysis, PEC designs allow more precise control over the interface and can achieve higher efficiencies. However, stability under operating conditions and the cost of complex materials remain major challenges.

3.3.3 PV-powered electrolysis (PV–EC)

The most commercially advanced way to produce green hydrogen today is to couple conventional solar PV systems with water electrolysers, such as proton-exchange membrane (PEM) or alkaline electrolysers. This modular PV–electrolysis configuration benefits from mature PV technology and continuously improving electrolyser performance.

3.3.4 Solar thermochemical hydrogen (STCH)

Solar thermochemical hydrogen production uses high-temperature heat from concentrated sunlight to drive redox cycles in metal oxides, such as ceria-based two-step water splitting cycles. In a typical cycle, a metal oxide is reduced at high temperature, releasing oxygen; at a lower temperature it is re-oxidised by steam (and sometimes carbon dioxide), producing hydrogen (and carbon monoxide).

STCH can in principle achieve high efficiencies and integrate thermal storage, but it requires advanced materials and reactor designs and is still in the early stages of development.

4. Current Global Capacity and Role

Solar energy has become the fastest-growing source of new electricity capacity worldwide. According to international statistics, global solar PV capacity crossed roughly 1.6 terawatts (TW) by the end of 2023 and has continued to grow rapidly since then.

Looking at the broader renewable mix, global renewable power capacity reached around 4,448 gigawatts (GW) by the end of 2024, after a record annual addition of about 585 GW. Solar PV accounted for the majority of this expansion, reflecting both its falling costs and its ease of deployment.

In terms of electricity generation, solar contributed roughly 6.9% of global electricity in 2024, up from around 5–6% the year before. In absolute terms, solar generation exceeded 2,000 terawatt-hours (TWh), and the year-on-year increase in solar generation was larger than for any other single source of electricity.

Some countries and regions already see much higher shares, with solar providing more than a quarter of annual electricity in certain markets and even higher shares during sunny months. These real-world examples provide valuable insights into how high-solar power systems can operate.

5. Current Research and Development

Solar R&D spans fundamental materials science, device engineering, system integration and energy policy. Several areas are particularly active today.

5.1 Next-generation photovoltaic materials

For crystalline silicon, research focuses on advanced cell architectures such as TOPCon and heterojunction designs that squeeze out the remaining efficiency gains while reducing material use and manufacturing costs. Silicon cells are approaching their practical single-junction efficiency limits, so attention is increasingly turning to tandem structures.

Perovskite–silicon tandem cells have set a series of efficiency records, with certified research cells achieving conversion efficiencies in the mid-30% range. At the module level, perovskite–silicon tandem panels with around 25% efficiency have been announced, indicating that commercialisation is beginning to follow the rapid progress seen in the lab.

5.2 Solar fuel technologies

For solar-to-hydrogen pathways, progress is being made on several fronts:

  • Photocatalysis: New catalyst compositions, nanostructuring strategies and reactor designs aim to raise solar-to-hydrogen efficiency and improve stability, while keeping materials abundant and safe.
  • Photoelectrochemical cells: Researchers are developing better protective coatings, interface engineering techniques and tandem PEC architectures that combine multiple absorbers for higher voltages and improved performance.
  • Solar thermochemical cycles: Work on ceria and other redox materials seeks to lower the peak operating temperatures, enhance heat recuperation and improve cycle efficiency, making STCH more practical.
  • PV-coupled electrolysis: System-level research optimises the interaction between PV arrays and electrolysers, including direct coupling strategies, dynamic operation, and techno-economic analyses for different locations and electricity price patterns.

5.3 System integration and grid flexibility

As solar penetration rises, the focus shifts from simply building more capacity to integrating variable solar power into the broader energy system. Key R&D topics include:

  • Advanced energy storage technologies, including battery systems, pumped hydro and thermal storage.
  • Smart grids and demand-response schemes that align flexible loads (such as electric vehicle charging and heat pumps) with solar output.
  • Grid-forming power electronics that help maintain stability in systems with fewer conventional synchronous generators.
  • Improved solar forecasting and digital optimisation tools that help operators schedule generation and storage more effectively.

6. Future Targets and Scenarios

Climate goals under the Paris Agreement imply rapid and sustained growth of solar energy. Many countries have endorsed an objective to triple global renewable power capacity by 2030 relative to 2023 levels.

Analyses by international agencies suggest that to keep a 1.5 °C temperature limit within reach, global renewable power capacity may need to exceed 11,000 GW by 2030, with solar and wind providing the bulk of new additions. This would require annual renewable capacity additions significantly larger than today’s record levels, but current solar growth trends are already moving in that direction.

Longer-term net-zero pathways to 2050 typically show solar becoming the single largest source of electricity worldwide sometime in the 2030s or early 2040s. In many scenarios, solar generation increases several-fold compared with today and supplies not only direct electricity demand but also large volumes of electricity for producing green hydrogen and synthetic fuels.

Sector by sector, solar is expected to:

  • Dominate new power capacity additions, especially in sunny regions with good land and grid access.
  • Provide cheap electricity for green hydrogen to decarbonise steel, chemicals and some forms of transport.
  • Deliver low- and medium-temperature heat for industry, reducing reliance on fossil boilers.
  • Enable net-zero or net-positive energy buildings through rooftop and building-integrated PV, combined with efficiency measures.

7. Challenges and Opportunities

Despite impressive progress, scaling solar energy to the levels envisioned in climate scenarios presents important challenges—and corresponding opportunities for innovation and policy design.

7.1 Variability and grid integration

Solar power is inherently variable, so high penetration levels require:

  • Adequate flexibility resources such as storage, demand response and flexible conventional generation.
  • Investment in transmission networks to move power from resource-rich regions to demand centres.
  • Market rules and regulations that reward flexibility and allow new business models to emerge.

7.2 Land use and environmental impacts

Utility-scale solar farms can require significant land areas, raising concerns about impacts on landscapes, agriculture and biodiversity. These can be mitigated by:

  • Using rooftops, car parks, brownfields and other previously disturbed land
  • Developing agrovoltaic systems that combine crop production with elevated solar panels
  • Deploying floating PV on reservoirs and other water bodies where appropriate
  • Careful planning to avoid ecologically sensitive areas and to integrate projects with local communities

7.3 Materials, supply chains and recycling

Solar technologies depend on materials such as silicon, glass, aluminium, copper and silver, and in some cases rarer elements like indium or tellurium. As deployment scales to multiple terawatts, issues of critical materials, supply chain resilience and end-of-life management become more important.

Ongoing work aims to reduce the use of scarce materials, diversify supply sources and develop economically viable recycling processes for PV modules and battery systems. By mid-century, recycled materials could become a noteworthy secondary resource for the solar industry.

7.4 Equity and access

Solar energy can promote energy access and social equity, but only if barriers are addressed. Off-grid solar systems and mini-grids can provide first-time electricity access to remote communities. Rooftop and community solar can lower bills and give households more control, but financing and policy design must ensure that low-income consumers are not left behind or disproportionately burdened.

8. Conclusion

In just a few decades, solar energy has moved from the margins of the power system to its centre. Today, solar PV capacity stands in the multi-terawatt range and is growing by hundreds of gigawatts each year. Solar already provides a substantial and rapidly increasing share of global electricity, and its role will only expand as costs continue to fall and complementary technologies such as storage and green hydrogen mature.

Next-generation PV materials, coupled with advances in solar-driven hydrogen production and efficient system integration, offer the prospect of an energy system in which clean, abundant sunlight is a primary driver of economic activity. Achieving this vision will require sustained investment, supportive policies and thoughtful planning, but the trajectory is clear: solar energy is set to become one of the defining infrastructures of the twenty-first century.
